Chapter 1 The skeleton includes all the structures that function to absorb energy without deforming or breaking, transfer energy, or store-and release energy

Most people (even many biologists!) think only of the bones when they think of the skeleton but skeletal structures also include the cartilages, tendons, ligaments, the dermis of the skin, and the connective tissues of the walls of the heart, the blood vessels, respiratory, and other fluid filled tubes in the body. Even fluids can sometimes act as skeletal elements (a hydrostatic, or hydrostatic skeleton). Indeed, any structure that functions to support and physically protect the body and to transfer, store, or absorb mechanical energy is part of the skeleton. The bones as organs have other, non-skeletal, functions, for example and homeostasis, the storage of fatty acids, and the site of the development of blood cells.
